Motivation

Qudini is a service for customer service management. The basic idea is that instead of letting your (you - being a popular store owner) customers stand in line, they could queue virtually through their phone. This both frees up time for the customer and makes life much less stressful for the staff.

Nursing a full-blown hatred for wasting time in a line, the idea behind Qudini really got me interested. After having a chat with the founders I knew this was where I had to go. I joined as the first hire and were lucky enough to follow a fledgling startup grow from office to office, turning the first pounds of profit and being influential in building a team.

Motivation.

I've always been very fond of the Aristotle quote:

We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit.

I developed Strive as a personal helper for me to keep good habits around and to force bad ones away.

List of habits, with some built in tips for first time users.

The Idea.

As this is in no way a novel idea, I decided on as simple an implementation as possible. Let the mechanics (streaks), be easily understandable and focus the development to be very well executed for that minimal feature set. This is my favorite way to approach development.

Strive is based on a streak mechanic, where after each day a habit is performed and ticked off in the app it accumulates the streak. If you fail to tick one day - you lose your streak. Another restriction of the app is that the habits can and must be ticked once and only once a day, there is no special habits for weekly or monthly activity. This is to limit the complexity and focus on habits that can be performed simply and frequently.

The app is on the android play store

Monitors are use to record information - to make sure the habits are effective.

Tech.

  • Cordova - Specifically the chrome apps for mobile fork of it.
  • AngularJS - Not without its challenges on mobile.
  • SASS - The SASS ruby compiler along with some compass plugins allows me to develop really modular CSS.
  • HTML5 - Some of the APIs used: localStorage and Web Animation API.
  • NodeJS - Backend is developed in NodeJS.
  • MongoDB - First experience with MongoDB.
  • Google Cloud Computing - Using GCP to manage VMs and deployment was a breeze.
  • Git - Good ol' git never fails.
  • Adobe Fireworks - For designing the screens.
  • Karma - Making sure every component does what it is supposed to.
  • Gulp & Grunt - For minification and spritesheet generation.

Strive was incredibly fun to build because it involved everything from developing the idea to dealing with backend deployment with Google Cloud.

Offline.

When dealing with a time sensitive thing as requiring users to tick habits before the day is over, the users access to a connection becomes an issue. I decided to face this problem head-on and develop Strive to be able to deal with offline mode completely - not just read only, but actually interacting and performing state changing actions when the app was offline.

I managed this by encoding actions and adding them to a queue that could be synced with the NodeJS backend. The system handles conflicts by always deferring to the newest action, it also silently rejects actions not inline with the current data model. I call this system a SyncModel.

A demonstration of Sync Model. The right side application is syncing over to the left one - this works even if they are both offline when the action was performed.

Summary.

Strive is my first attempt at a hybrid app and I've learned a lot from my experiences. I developed a new respect for web performance, both the tricks and hacks to achieve it and the art that is performance debugging. Oh yea, and the chrome dev tools team rocks!

Motivation

Piction is a service that takes your image and adds survey type questions to it in a page which you can then share to anywhere on the web. Its used as a very light weight survey tool when you need peoples input on something you’ve made. This project sprung up as a side project from Photic.me as a lightweight alternative for people wanting to use their own network to get feedback.

We really like simple messages.

 

How it works.

We love simplicity in our projects and Piction, is a very clear example of that. A simple welcome screen directs you through the upload / linking part in a smooth, well tested process. After you’ve got your image into place you proceed by creating some questions to go along with it.

The front page when logged in

There are simple comment questions, rating questions and choices questions. There is also the option to add a comparison image. After you’ve decided on the questions you click Finish to create your survey page, from which we help you with sharing options. And that’s it, you now have your own survey page.

The Image finder in action

Tech.

Piction is based on CodeIgniter, our favorite PHP-framework. The site basically involves three parts – the front page, the questions creator page and the final page. The front page consists of an ajax based registration, a custom build JavaScript interactive slideshow. Also worth noticing here is the image crawler which we built from scratch.

The question creation page

The lower part of the question creation page

The question creator page is a very heavy custom image JavaScript UI to provide all the survey controls as well as provide a smooth transition between states. The third and last page is the page where the feedback is given, this is based of our Photic engine with some heavy tweaking to integrate the javscript survery controls and an ajax based comment module.

Some questions

The comparison voting input

Summary.

Piction was an interesting approach and many of our users had never seen the concept. Innovation is funny that way, it may seem obvious for a web developer that there are many traditional things we do on the web that would be very easy to improve – but its not always that easy. Piction at first had a very innovative registration process that did almost everything without the user even noticing it – when you had submitted your image you all of a sudden had a new account. We thought, this is surely easier than that old fashioned register form.

The final survey page

But to our surprise our users did not like it at all. There was a lot of confusion and the lack of transparency caused them to doubt the service. People were actually looking for the old fashioned registration box even though we told them they didn’t have too. Innovation is a careful balance between old habits and new ways.

The answer form

 

Piction taught us a lot about innovation and user perception – which is everything. We want to thank our user base for contributing and ironing out all the details to produce the refined beta version we now have online at Piction.it. Thank you guys – we had a blast!

Motivation.

Now there are many photography communities out there and many of them do offer ways of feedback – generally through the tried and true comment-box. The quality of feedback however is seldom more than one line of praise, a “Wow, nice photo” or “Great pix!!”.

For some I’m sure this is just enough, but there are photographers who would rather have someones honest and specific opinion. This is why we decided to create Photic. To create a place where you could get feedback from photographers you respected – to grow as an artist.

How it Works.

You sign up for an account and are then greeted by the photo feed, where all the requests (the photos that are up for critique) are listed. By giving your opinion on another persons photography you can be awarded stars (provided the photographer behind the photo think’s your opinion was helpful). Conversely If you upload a photo you’ll get 5 stars which you can award the people giving you critiques.

By giving helpful critiques and gathering stars, you earn titles and can unlock more features of the site. This way we reward our most helpful users and motivate them to stay on, as well as providing high quality advice to the people wanting feedback.

Another principle part in the system is the way that we modeled the critique giving. We made sure to employ as many psychological / UX strategies we could to make sure critiques are easy to give but also that they give a constructive impression and leaves the artist with a few new perspectives. Therefore the critique input is constructed out of 3 questions – What was your first Impression?, What did you like? and What would you do different? Answering questions makes the writing much easier and by first acknowledging what a critic thought was good about a photo, makes the critiquee more open to the advice.

This system has so far helped to produce some very high quality critiques and helps photographers improve their skills.

Technology.

The heart and brain of Photic is created in CodeIgniter, which is hands down our favorite PHP framework – hats of to Ellis labs.

This wassnt our biggest project, but it still had a lot of different modules to make it work. The thing to note when you are developing your own applications, and building a user base is that things like analytics and channels for feedback has to be custom designed too. Among the many things that power Photic, big components are:

  • Ajax based live chat
  • Ajax image uploader and cropper
  • Star currency system connected to an auth-system for feature unlocking.
  • Critique system and a Profile.

Summary.

To sum it up, Photic was an amazing project that taught us a lot about usability philosophy, modular architecture, gamification and a lot of other things.

But most of all it taught us how important the end value is. That at the end of the day, if we can do only one thing – it should be to make sure that when a Photic user closes the lid of his laptop he should feel like he gained something. If our users gets value from what we do, we’re making the world better – even if its just a little bit.
